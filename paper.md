## Abstract

Drug discovery faces a creativity problem. Despite three decades of methodological progress in computational chemistry and the more recent transformation by deep learning, the underlying architectural commitments of drug-discovery AI remain narrow relative to what creativity research suggests is possible. AI systems for drug discovery have largely been built without engaging the substantial body of creativity research that formalizes how generative cognition actually works - concept generation, programme revision, multi-scale agency, deliberate incubation, analogical structure mapping. This paper argues that the gap between these literatures is not merely citational but architectural, and that closing it points toward concrete classes of AI systems that the field has not yet built.

The argument proceeds in three steps. First, an OpenAlex-based bibliometric study of 53,792 papers (2017-2026) spanning drug-discovery AI, chemistry-AI methodology, protein-AI, materials informatics, and computational chemistry establishes that engagement between the two literatures is rare and not improving. Twenty-two corpus papers (0.041%) cite any of thirty canonical creativity-research figures; three substantive engagements appear, all at the biology-AI-philosophy frontier rather than in core methodology. The proportional citation rate is stable across three corpus iterations despite the largest being 74% larger than the smallest, ruling out the explanation that the engagement rate is an artifact of corpus boundaries. Kuhn appears in 411 corpus abstracts as "paradigm shift" with zero Kuhn citations - a quantification of vocabulary reinvention without literature engagement.

Second, a structured 31 by 14 matrix maps creativity frameworks (126 frameworks across 31 sub-clusters) against the AI approaches in current use (14 categories), producing 434 cells classified by implementation status. Hybrid neurosymbolic architecture is empty across all 31 sub-clusters under strict reading; held-out validation on 8 systems not used in matrix construction reproduces the pattern. Multi-scale biological agency and deliberate incubation are similarly under-instantiated. These are not gaps in the field's ambition - they are gaps in the field's architectural toolkit.

Third, six research-derived architectural directions translate the matrix's empty cells into specifiable AI systems: a neurosymbolic core integrating programme revision and multi-scale agency; a structure-mapping substrate for analogical discovery; a field-aware architecture; a constraint-engineering substrate; a stigmergic distributed architecture; an incubation-aware system. One component (programme-progressivity tracking) is operationalized to depth across six historical drug-development cases (imatinib, trastuzumab, CFTR modulators, amyloid-cascade, anti-VEGF, tau-targeting) demonstrating the analytical reach of the framework on clean-progressive, productively-pivoting, threshold-contested, split-outcome, and degenerative outcomes.

The implications are pragmatic. If the architectural reading is correct, the path to substantially better drug-discovery AI runs through architectures the field has not yet implemented rather than through further scaling of architectures it already deploys. The empirical citation gap and matrix-based architectural analysis are this paper's primary findings. The six architectural directions are testable predictions for future work, not demonstrated performance results - but they specify what the work would look like in enough detail to begin.

**Keywords:** drug discovery, creativity research, AI architecture, neurosymbolic AI, multi-scale biology, computational creativity, bibliometric analysis, programme-progressivity tracking.

## 1. Introduction

Drug-discovery AI is in an architectural moment. The field has assembled remarkable capabilities over the past decade - protein-structure prediction at near-experimental accuracy, generative chemistry at scale, agentic systems coordinating multi-step experimental campaigns. The trajectory is impressive and the published methodology genuinely advances. But the architectural variety on display is narrower than the field's framing suggests. Read across the methodology literature, the dominant pattern is variations on a small number of architectural commitments: deep neural networks (predictive or generative) optionally combined with reinforcement learning, retrieval, search, or active learning loops. What is largely missing is engagement with the substantial body of research on how generative cognition actually works - the creativity-research tradition spanning Boden's combinatorial-exploratory-transformational taxonomy, Wallas's stage model, Wiggins's R/T/E formalism, Hofstadter's fluid analogies, Csikszentmihalyi's systems model, Gentner's structure-mapping, Lakatos's research-programme dynamics, Levin's multi-scale agency, and the philosophical and historical scholarship that surrounds them.

This paper makes three claims of progressively weaker evidential strength. The first is empirical: at scale, drug-discovery AI literature does not engage substantively with this body of work. The second is inferential: that absence is not merely citational but architectural - the AI systems that result from this disengagement have committed to a narrower method space than they otherwise would. The third is forward-looking: the empty cells in that method space correspond to specifiable AI architectures the field could build, several of which the paper specifies at engineering-grade depth.

The implications are practical, not philosophical. If the architectural reading is correct, the path to substantially better drug-discovery AI runs through architectures the field has not yet implemented. The work this paper specifies is not a critique of current methods but an inventory of methods that the field could be, but is not yet, pursuing.

### 1.2 The citation gap

The shape of contemporary drug-discovery AI reflects specific historical contingencies. Three are particularly consequential.

**Training data and benchmark gravity.** The field's training data and benchmarks reward predictive accuracy on existing labels (binding affinity, ADMET endpoints, trial outcomes); they do not measure or reward creativity-relevant outputs (concept-evolution traces, cross-scale negotiation, programme-revision reasoning). The substrate has shaped the architectures. GuacaMol, MoleculeNet, MOSES, and Therapeutics Data Commons define progress operationally — what the field can measure determines what the field optimizes — and what these benchmarks measure is shaped by what was easy to instrument over the past decade.

**Post-2012 deep-learning generational discontinuity.** The current shape of drug-discovery AI was largely set by the deep-learning revolution post-2012 (AlphaFold lineage; molecular-property-prediction lineage; generative chemistry lineage). The pre-deep-learning generation (expert systems including MYCIN-derivatives; SOAR/ACT-R applications to medicine; symbolic-reasoning architectures) had different architectural commitments — symbolic, rule-based, multi-component, hybrid. The discontinuity between pre-2012 and post-2012 work means the current deep-learning-dominated drug-discovery AI literature has few documented failed attempts at the creativity-derived architectures the matrix surfaces. The symbolic-substrate work that connects most naturally to creativity research was orphaned rather than integrated. Computational creativity research (Wiggins, Boden, Hofstadter, Colton, Veale) was in the orphaned tradition.

**Community-non-bridging.** The communities developing creativity research and the community developing drug-discovery AI cite each other rarely. To test this empirically, this analysis conducted a structured systematic survey of citations across major drug-discovery AI venues (full methodology and results in supplementary `citation_network_analysis.md`). The survey selected 100 papers across five categories — drug-discovery AI reviews from 2017-2026; AI methodology papers for molecular generation, repurposing, and property prediction; agentic-AI-for-science systems including those explicitly invoking creativity-related framing (Coscientist, ChemCrow, Krenn's "artificial muses" program); AI for biology and medicine adjacent venues (AlphaFold, ESM, scGPT, Geneformer, PrimeKG, Hetionet, RFdiffusion); and high-impact generative AI in chemistry. The survey checked each paper for direct citations to eleven foundational creativity-research figures (Wiggins, Boden, Hofstadter, Lakatos, Wallas, Csikszentmihalyi, Gentner, Fauconnier-Turner, Finke-Ward-Smith, Koestler, Levin).

**Result of n=100 survey: zero direct citations to the eleven canonical figures across 100 papers.** The pattern is consistent across all five categories. The strongest single piece of evidence is Krenn's "artificial muses" research program — a substantial physics-discovery research program at the Max Planck Institute for the Science of Light, with the 2022 *Nature Reviews Physics* paper "On scientific understanding with artificial intelligence" and subsequent papers and talks, explicitly framed around creativity terminology. The 2022 paper cites Boden's *Mind as Machine* (2008) in a cognitive-science / AI-history framing context, and Krenn & Zeilinger 2020 cites a computational-creativity edited volume in passing; neither paper engages substantively with the canonical creativity-process frameworks. The pattern across the n=100 sample, calibrated by direct verification of representative cases, is not absolute absence of all creativity-adjacent references but absence of sustained engagement with the canonical creativity-process literature. AI-for-science researchers reaching for creativity-related concepts (Krenn's "artificial muses"; Coscientist's "(semi-)autonomous experimental design"; agentic-science taxonomies of "autonomy levels" structurally similar to but not citing Boden's combinatorial/exploratory/transformational distinctions) tend to develop operational frameworks de novo rather than building on the canonical creativity-process apparatus.

**Bibliometric extension at scale: pattern preserved across corpus boundary expansions.** The n=100 survey was extended into a formal API-driven bibliometric analysis using OpenAlex (full methodology and findings in supplementary `bibliometric_findings_v3.md` and `citation_network_analysis.md` Section 8). The extension was executed in three iterations to test the corpus-boundary objection from hostile review. v1 used a strict drug-discovery filter (53,792 papers; 0.041% engagement). v2 expanded to broader chemistry-AI methodology (33,000 papers; 0.045% engagement; superseded by v3). v3 added protein-AI, materials informatics, computational chemistry + ML, and QSAR/cheminformatics terms — capturing AlphaFold, RFdiffusion, Coscientist, ChemCrow, Schwaller's Molecular Transformer, Burger 2020 mobile robotic chemist, ProteinMPNN, and similar named canonicals that v1 and v2 missed due to keyword-filter narrowness. The v3 corpus is 53,792 papers (74% larger than v1).

The figure set was extended in all three iterations from eleven to thirty figures across thirty-four individuals, adding nineteen extensions covering creativity-relevant traditions a hostile reviewer would name as absent from the original eleven (Simon, Newell, Amabile, Sternberg, Campbell, Simonton, Schön, Alexander, Altshuller, Stokes, Hutchins, Clark, Chalmers, Latour, Knorr-Cetina, Kuhn, Feyerabend, Kauffman, Kaufman, Runco).

The v3 calibrated headline: across the 53,792-paper corpus, 22 papers (0.041%) cite at least one of the thirty canonical creativity figures; 3 substantive engagements per heuristic classification, all at the biology-AI-philosophy frontier (Chalmers/extended-mind in AI-DD epistemology chapter; Latour twice in molecular-recognition philosophy and OMICS systems-science editorial); 19 are passing references in materials-science, GenAI-methodology, network-biology, and synthetic-biology contexts. Eleven of 30 figures appear at all. The four extended-set figures (Simon, Newell, Feyerabend, Alexander) that emerge at v3 corpus scale all appear in passing — none as creativity-framework adoptions. The proportional citation rate is stable at 0.041–0.045% across v1, v2, and v3 corpus expansions despite v3 being 74% larger than v1, ruling out the alternative explanation that the engagement rate is an artifact of strict corpus boundary. The parallel-vocabulary phenomenon is robust at v3 scale: Kuhn's "paradigm shift" appears in 411 v3-corpus abstracts (~0.8% of corpus) with zero Kuhn citations among them — the cleanest single empirical quantification of reinvention without attribution.

**Counterexamples exist and have been catalogued.** Shahhosseini et al. 2025 (*Large Language Models for Scientific Idea Generation: A Creativity-Centered Survey*, arXiv:2511.07448) explicitly cites Boden 2004 and Rhodes' 4Ps framework. This is a real bridge paper at the LLM-for-scientific-ideation level; it does not penetrate drug-discovery primary literature within the sample examined. The bibliometric extension surfaced one additional clean bridge in adjacent chemistry-AI methodology literature: Coley, Eyke, and Jensen 2020 (*Autonomous Discovery in the Chemical Sciences—Part II: Outlook*, *Angewandte Chemie International Edition* 59(52):23414-23436) co-cites Boden 1998 ("Creativity and artificial intelligence") with chemistry-AI canonical methods. Coley 2020 was excluded from the v1 strict primary corpus due to "autonomous discovery in chemistry" framing rather than "drug discovery"; v3 includes it. Its existence calibrates the empirical claim: the pattern is "little sustained engagement with the canonical creativity-process literature in drug-discovery AI specifically, with isolated bridges in adjacent chemistry-AI literature (Coley 2020 → Boden 1998) and isolated late-2025 LLM-for-scientific-ideation survey work (Shahhosseini 2025 → Boden 2004 + Rhodes' 4Ps)."

**The strongest objection: convergent reinvention versus missed opportunity.** A hostile reading is that absent citations to creativity-research figures do not establish architectural impoverishment. Drug-discovery AI uses different intellectual lineages — medicinal chemistry, design-make-test-analyze cycles, Bayesian optimization, active learning, optimal experimental design, causal discovery, multi-objective optimization, model-based reinforcement learning, de novo molecular design, systems pharmacology — and may instantiate creativity-relevant functions through these lineages without citing creativity theory. On this reading, the parallel-vocabulary phenomenon evidences convergent reinvention under domain-appropriate terms rather than missed opportunity.

The objection lands at the level of partial functional reinvention but not at the level of architectural patterns the matrix surfaces as universally absent. The matrix's Type 1 EMPTY classifications are claims that no implementation exists at all under strict reading; convergent reinvention does not apply where the construct is genuinely absent. The X9 (neurosymbolic) universal Type 1 EMPTY finding is therefore not vulnerable to the convergent-reinvention objection in the way that the parallel-vocabulary observations are; held-out validation reproduces the X9 finding for 7 of 8 systems, with one borderline PARTIAL classification (RUGGED) flagged honestly. The citation gap is documented; the architectural-gap inference is structurally supported by the matrix and partly validated against held-out systems but depends on the matrix's classifications being right; the performance-gap claim is speculative and requires implementation work this paper specifies but does not execute.

**Acknowledged limits of the citation-network analysis.** Several limitations affect interpretation. The sample is biased toward English-language well-indexed venues; industry-internal documents and non-English literature are not represented. Reference list inspection in the bibliometric extension uses OpenAlex's `referenced_works` field, which is incomplete for ~24% of the v3 primary corpus (mostly recent papers and arXiv preprints whose reference lists OpenAlex hasn't fully ingested) — direct-citation counts are therefore a lower bound. Conceptual presence is partially captured by the bibliometric extension's fuzzy-concept scan (the Kuhn finding of 411 paradigm-shift uses with zero Kuhn citations is the cleanest single quantification), but full conceptual engagement requires close reading that automated scans cannot match. The extended-figure-set selection is broader than the original eleven but is not exhaustive; a hostile reviewer could propose figures not in this set, though the pattern across v1, v2, and v3 (most extended-set figures absent or in passing-reference contexts) suggests further expansion would not change the methodology-layer architectural-impoverishment inference. The bibliometric extension's classification of substantive vs passing engagement is heuristic and single-classifier; full-text inspection would tighten it. Older creativity works are split across multiple OpenAlex work-records (Kuhn, Wallas, Koestler, Alexander affected by edition fragmentation). The bibliometric extension is sufficient to support the calibrated citation-gap claim with appropriate caveats; the architectural-gap inference remains the work's middle-strength claim.

**A note on what the gap means in cost terms.** Drug discovery cost structure is dominated by wet-lab synthesis, assay, and clinical development; computational generation and reasoning are a small fraction of that cost. (Schneider et al. 2020 provides a recent treatment of AI-era drug-design cost and bottleneck context; precise dollar shares vary across discovery-stage and indication and are not reproduced here.) The architectural opportunities the matrix surfaces optimize the computational layer; their value comes from producing *better discovery decisions* given the wet-lab cost structure rather than from changing the wet-lab cost itself. Some hypotheses (P1's CSNP that surfaces cross-scale conflicts before wet-lab investment) are well-aligned with this cost structure; others optimize aspects that are not the bottleneck. Section 11 returns to this with operational realism throughout.

The historical contingencies — substrate and benchmark gravity; deep-learning generational discontinuity; community-non-bridging — together suggest the architectural conversation is path-dependent rather than the result of considered rejection. The literature hasn't been examined and dismissed; it hasn't been examined. The gap is potentially closable rather than necessarily closed.

### 1.3 The matrix as diagnostic instrument

The matrix is a diagnostic instrument that operationalizes the architectural-gap inference. It surfaces specifically where the gap is — which combinations of creativity-framework commitments and AI-method capabilities have been built; which have not been built; which cannot be built (where computational translation resists). The matrix is what lets the contribution — empirical citation gap, structurally inferred architectural gap, derived testable hypotheses — be made specifically rather than generically.

A 31 × 14 matrix is constructed where rows are sub-clusters of creativity frameworks (organized into four major clusters: ontology, mechanism, conditions, scope) and columns are categories of AI approaches to medical discovery (predictive scoring, generative molecular design, retrieval, evolutionary search, RL, multi-agent, self-refining, knowledge graphs, neurosymbolic, active learning, foundation models, causal AI, multi-modal, simulation). Each of the 434 cells receives a status assessment with explicit thresholds for IMPLEMENTED (primary architectural commitment), PARTIAL (partially instantiated with significant limitations), INCIDENTAL (functionally present but not deliberate), and EMPTY (with sub-classification into Type 1 — engineerable but untried; Type 4 — resists computational translation; Type 2 and Type 3 also defined but unused in this matrix).

The matrix population is followed by drill-down treatment for high-leverage Type 1 EMPTY cells, producing implementation sketches at hypothesis-grade depth. The drill-down work surfaces architectural patterns that are uniformly absent from current medical-discovery AI but well-specified by creativity research. Six architectural hypotheses are then derived from the drill-down findings, each composing a coherent design from cells that share architectural commitments.

The matrix produces three kinds of output:

- **Empirical findings about where the architectural gap is most concentrated.** In the original matrix, X9 (neurosymbolic) is Type 1 EMPTY in all 31 of 31 sub-clusters under strict reading; held-out systems preserve the pattern with one borderline KG-RAG exception (RUGGED) that forces threshold examination. D4 (multi-scale biological) is the densest single Type 1 row; B4 (incubation) shows no deliberate-incubation architecture across the 14 columns examined (8 Type 1, 3 INCIDENTAL upgrade-targets, 3 Type 4 honest-empties); Cluster C (conditions) and Cluster D Pass 1 (biological scope) have zero IMPLEMENTED cells across all AI categories. These findings ground the architectural-gap inference specifically rather than generically.

- **Engineerable opportunities.** Each Type 1 cell represents an architectural pattern that creativity research specifies clearly enough to implement and that no current medical-discovery AI system has implemented. The hypotheses (Sections 5-10) compose these opportunities into coherent architectures.

- **Honest acknowledgment of non-translation.** Type 4 cells (90 across the matrix) document where computational translation appears to fail — where a creativity framework's commitments resist mapping onto current AI methods. The Type 4 cells are not engineering targets; they are honest records of where translation does not work, which prevents the thesis from claiming more tractability than the literature supports.

The hypotheses are existence proofs that translation produces specifiable architectures for specific framework clusters. P1 translates Wiggins's R/T/E formalism, Lakatos's protective-belt structure, and Levin's TAME multi-scale agency into a five-component neurosymbolic architecture (SRS, NGIS, MSAS, CSNP, PPT). P2 translates Geneplore preinventive structures, Copycat fluid-concept dynamics, structure-mapping, and conceptual blending into a four-component analogical-discovery substrate (SNBC, PSL, CWGE, BE). P3, P4, P5, P6 translate other framework clusters. The collective output is not that any single architecture is best, nor that translated architectures will outperform existing systems; it is that systematic translation produces multiple distinct, specifiable architectures whose claims are testable.

### 1.4 Scope and author positioning

**Scope by discovery function.** Drug discovery is multiple epistemic structures with different binding constraints. The architectural hypotheses derived from the matrix are most likely to matter for high-uncertainty discovery tasks where data is sparse and decision quality matters more than predictive accuracy on existing labels: mechanism generation, cross-scale translational risk, programme revision and protective-belt reasoning, field-aware prioritization, analogical repurposing, deliberate incubation in long-horizon work. They are weakest where data is abundant and bitter-lesson dynamics may dominate: standard property prediction on well-populated benchmarks, single-target binding-affinity scoring, retrosynthesis route prediction. **Author positioning.** This work is the product of a single author (Maxwell, AIMR) with research-operations background in clinical research enterprise consulting. The positioning shapes the analysis in three ways that warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, the AI-categorization (Axis 2) reflects deployment-relevant boundaries between AI methods rather than purely academic taxonomy; a pure-AI-research framing might categorize differently (e.g., by neural architecture type rather than by mechanism-of-creativity-engagement). Third, the matrix's high-leverage-cell findings reflect what the author found salient; a reviewer with different background would likely populate cells consistently with the author at the threshold-application level, but emphasize different findings as most consequential. The matrix is the work of a single author throughout; further review would likely shift individual classifications at margins and could surface additional hypotheses from the same gap analysis.

## 2. Matrix construction

### 2.1 Axis 1: creativity frameworks

The creativity research literature is large enough that any organization is selective. The Axis 1 organization adopted here is by *type of claim the framework makes about creativity* rather than by historical lineage or by named-author taxonomy. Four clusters with explicit thresholds:

- **Cluster A (Ontology)** — frameworks that make claims about *what creativity is* at the ontological level. Includes search-based ontologies (Boden, Wiggins, Kauffman, Schmidhuber), recombination ontologies (Koestler, Mednick, Fauconnier-Turner, Campbell), emergence ontologies (Levin, autopoiesis, niche construction, major transitions), difference-production ontologies (Deleuze, Bergson, Whitehead), paradigm-change ontologies (Kuhn, Lakatos, Feyerabend), and discovery-vs-invention ontologies (Heidegger, Polanyi). 6 sub-clusters.

- **Cluster B (Mechanism)** — frameworks that make claims about *how creativity operates* at the cognitive or computational mechanism level. Includes stage models (Wallas, Poincaré, Hadamard, Geneplore, design thinking), dual-process and divergent-convergent (Guilford, Amabile, dual-process theory, DMN salience network), analogy and structure-mapping (Gentner, Hofstadter Copycat, Holyoak-Thagard, Nersessian), unconscious and incubation (Kris, Winnicott, Milner, Sio-Ormerod), iterative refinement and dialectic (Lakatos, Schön, active inference), quantitative and statistical (Simonton, Schmidhuber, flow), and computational and synthetic mechanisms (GANs, VAEs, diffusion, LLM-evolutionary, multi-agent LLM, self-refine). 7 sub-clusters.

- **Cluster C (Conditions)** — frameworks that make claims about *what enables or constrains creativity*. Includes motivation and affect (flow, intrinsic motivation, investment theory, affect modulation), constraint as generative (Stravinsky, Oulipo, Cage, Eno), dialogue and otherness (Bakhtin, Buber, Vygotsky, Rogers), social structure and field (Csikszentmihalyi, Burt, Sawyer, Rogers diffusion), contemplative and altered states (beginner's mind, wu wei, psychedelic, hayrah, indigenous), environment and material conditions (Hennessey-Amabile environment, Clark-Chalmers, Galison-Latour), and humanistic conditions for self-actualization (Maslow, May, autotelic). 7 sub-clusters.

- **Cluster D (Scope)** — frameworks that make claims about *the substrate or scale at which creativity operates*. Pass 1 (biological scales): cellular and molecular cognition, plant cognition, animal cognition and innovation, multi-scale biological systems (Levin TAME, holobiont, evo-devo, horizontal transfer), ecosystem-level creativity. Pass 2 (non-biological / synthetic / hybrid): group and team creativity, organizational and institutional, cultural and civilizational, synthetic creativity (AI as agent), human-AI hybrid, swarm and collective intelligence. 11 sub-clusters total.

Each sub-cluster contains 2–6 individual frameworks. The total framework count is 126 frameworks across 31 sub-clusters (verified by parsing all framework specifications across cluster files; preliminary counts during drafting were approximations). Matrix population operates at the sub-cluster level (31 rows), not at the individual-framework level — sub-cluster status is the most permissive status across constituent frameworks, with confidence markers indicating where the rating depends on a specific implementable-kernel framework within the sub-cluster.

**Two scope clarifications about which creativity is at stake.** First, the 4C model (Kaufman & Beghetto, 2009) distinguishes mini-c (transformative learning experiences), little-c (everyday creativity), Pro-c (professional-level expertise) and Big-C (eminent creativity). Medical-discovery AI operates predominantly at Pro-c and Big-C levels — professional drug discovery and field-transforming therapeutic breakthroughs, respectively. Many of the frameworks the matrix draws on were developed in mini-c and little-c contexts (educational creativity, individual problem-solving). The mapping between framework-context and AI-application-context is not always seamless; some framework specifications need adaptation to translate from mini-c/little-c origins to Pro-c/Big-C medical-discovery targets. Second, Boden's distinction between P-creativity (psychologically novel, new to the creator) and H-creativity (historically novel, new to humanity) matters for AI systems specifically. Medical-discovery AI cares predominantly about H-creativity. Frameworks oriented toward P-creativity (some incubation research, some flow research) need H-creativity reinterpretation when ported to medical-discovery contexts. The matrix doesn't systematically tag cells by 4C level or P-vs-H orientation, but the hypotheses' implementation sketches implicitly target Pro-c / H-creativity contexts.

The cluster organization has tradeoffs. Some frameworks make claims at multiple levels (Csikszentmihalyi makes both motivation/affect claims and social-structure claims; Sternberg makes both motivation claims and investment-as-decision claims). For these, the framework is placed in its dominant cluster with cross-references to other clusters. The organization's value is in producing a manageable taxonomy that supports systematic gap analysis; alternative organizations are possible but the analytic findings are robust to reasonable variations.

### 2.2 Axis 2: AI approaches to medical discovery

The 14 AI-approach categories are organized by *what the systems mechanically do* rather than by where they sit in a discovery pipeline. The mechanism-organization is appropriate for matching against creativity frameworks (which are mechanism claims) and produces matrix discriminative-ness across columns.

The 14 categories: (X1) predictive scoring and property prediction; (X2) generative molecular design; (X3) retrieval and case-based reasoning; (X4) evolutionary and quality-diversity search; (X5) reinforcement learning with task-specific rewards; (X6) multi-agent debate, society, and ensemble systems; (X7) self-refining and self-critiquing loops; (X8) knowledge-graph reasoning and structured inference; (X9) hybrid neural-symbolic systems; (X10) active learning and experimental-design-in-the-loop; (X11) foundation-model adaptation to biomedical domains; (X12) causal inference and mechanism discovery; (X13) multi-modal integration; (X14) simulation-based discovery and digital twins.

Three structural commitments shape the categorization. First, mechanism organization rather than pipeline organization. Second, granularity tuned for matrix readability — each column distinct enough that cell statuses meaningfully differ across columns. Third, the substrate-vs-application tension acknowledged: foundation models (X11) and simulation (X14) have substrate flavor and could appear under multiple application columns; they are preserved as their own columns to surface their substrate-as-architectural-commitment role specifically. Cross-references handle the overlap explicitly.

Two boundary issues remain partially unresolved. (a) X6 (multi-agent) and X7 (self-refining) blur where a single agent runs internal critique cycles that look multi-agent. (b) X11 cross-cuts most other categories. Both are handled by classification by *primary architectural commitment* with cross-reference notation in cells where the same system instantiates multiple categories at different levels of commitment.

**On computational creativity research as prior implementation context.** Several creativity frameworks the matrix draws on have substantial computational implementations in non-medical domains, primarily through the International Conference on Computational Creativity (ICCC) tradition and adjacent venues. Wiggins's R/T/E formalism has been operationalized in computational creativity systems (Wiggins, 2006); Hofstadter's Copycat has been ported across letter-string, design, music, and shape domains (Hofstadter & FARG, 1995); Fauconnier-Turner conceptual blending has been operationalized through Goguen's algebraic blending and successors (Pereira, 2007); structure-mapping has a forty-year computational tradition (Gentner, 1989; Falkenhainer et al., 1989); Veale's metaphor and analogy work has implementation history (Veale, 2012); Colton's painting fool and similar systems have specifically engaged with creativity-framework instantiation (Colton & Wiggins, 2012). The matrix's "Type 1 EMPTY" status for medical-discovery applications of these frameworks does not mean the architectures are theoretical; it means medical-discovery applications specifically have not been built. The hypotheses in Sections 5–10 should be read with this context: the architectural patterns have implementation precedents in non-medical domains; the contribution is field-bridging to medical discovery, not architectural primary. The engineering burden is correspondingly real — porting Copycat from letter-strings to biomedical concepts is more substantial than the prior ports; porting structure-mapping from physics analogies to disease-mechanism analogies requires substantial domain adaptation. The hypotheses acknowledge this engineering burden in their respective implementation discussions.

### 2.3 Cell status definitions

Five status values with explicit thresholds:

- **IMPLEMENTED**: the column-method instantiates the row-cluster's central architectural claim as a deliberate, primary architectural commitment. The framework is not just present but *designed-in*. Threshold: a representative published system can be cited where the framework is named or its specifications are deliberately followed.

- **PARTIAL**: the column-method instantiates parts of the row-cluster's claim but with significant limitations relative to what the framework specifies. The framework is partly present, partly missing. Threshold: representative systems exist but framework specifications they don't satisfy are non-trivial.

- **INCIDENTAL**: the column-method has properties consistent with the row-cluster's framework but not as designed-in commitment. The framework is present functionally but absent architecturally. Threshold: the system's behavior could be described in framework terms post-hoc but the framework is not part of the design rationale.

- **EMPTY**: the row-cluster's claim is absent from the column-method's architectural commitments. Sub-classified into:
  - **Type 1**: engineerable but untried. The framework is operationally specified enough to support implementation; the implementation has not been done in medical discovery (it may exist in other domains).
  - **Type 2**: engineerable, partially tried with significant limitations. Reserved but unused — the matrix's actual cells with this character are coded as PARTIAL above the EMPTY threshold.
  - **Type 3**: engineerable but historically unsuccessful. Reserved but unused — the current deep-learning-dominated drug-discovery AI literature has few documented failed attempts at the specific creativity-derived architectures the matrix surfaces; almost all gaps are Type 1.
  - **Type 4**: resists computational translation in this column specifically. The framework's commitments are phenomenological, dispositional, or otherwise non-engineerable through this column's architectural patterns.

The status definitions are strict by design. The matrix's discriminative power comes from explicit thresholds rather than from charitable readings.

### 2.4 Sub-cluster status convention

Sub-cluster status is the *most permissive* status across constituent frameworks. If a sub-cluster contains four frameworks and three are Type 4 EMPTY but one is Type 1 EMPTY, the sub-cluster status is Type 1 EMPTY. This convention reflects that the matrix's analytic purpose is identifying where architectural opportunities exist; one engineerable framework within a sub-cluster suffices to make the cell engineerable. Confidence markers indicate where the rating depends on a single implementable-kernel framework rather than on the cluster as a whole.

The matrix is therefore an *opportunity map*, not a *framework-cluster translation map*. A Type 1 EMPTY sub-cluster status means at least one framework within the sub-cluster is engineerable but untried; it does not mean every framework in the sub-cluster is engineerable. Some sub-clusters with Type 1 status contain Type 4 frameworks that resist computational translation, and these resistances are masked by the permissive convention. The drill-down treatment in `matrix_drilldown.md` recovers framework-level granularity for high-leverage cells; readers wanting framework-level rather than sub-cluster-level claims should consult the drill-down.

### 2.5 Population strategy and methodological status

The matrix was populated row-by-row, one cluster per drafting block, with per-row preambles anchoring cell judgments and post-row notes flagging the most informative cells. Population was followed by independent verification: the populated tables were parsed programmatically to produce verified status counts, and the verified counts were checked against narrative summaries during drafting (with discrepancies flagged and reconciled).

**Population method.** The matrix was populated by a single reviewer drawing on multiple knowledge sources: medical-discovery AI literature (selected through targeted searches of arXiv, bioRxiv, *Nature Machine Intelligence*, *Drug Discovery Today*, *Bioinformatics*, and similar venues for specific framework × method combinations); creativity research literature (drawing on canonical sources within each framework cluster as documented in the cluster files); and deployed-system descriptions (vendor documentation, conference presentations, white papers from organizations including DeepMind, Insilico Medicine, Atomwise, BenevolentAI, Recursion). Inclusion criteria for IMPLEMENTED status required either a published peer-reviewed system that can be cited or a deployed system with documentation sufficient to verify the architectural claim. Systems described only in marketing materials without architectural specification were not credited as IMPLEMENTED.

**Methodology limits.** The matrix is the work of a single author throughout. Cell classifications were populated by one reviewer applying stated thresholds; the matrix has not been independently rated by another classifier. Individual classifications would likely shift at margins under further review (a few cells reclassified between PARTIAL and INCIDENTAL, or between Type 1 EMPTY and PARTIAL, depending on rater strictness), and the narrative findings would likely shift more substantially. The held-out validation (supplementary `preregistered_matrix_predictions.md`) tests methodology stability — pre-registered predictions matched cell-level classifications on 8 held-out systems — but uses the same author for pre-registration and classification, so it tests stability rather than inter-rater validity. Cross-LLM review passes informed several calibration revisions and surfaced specific issues (convergent-reinvention objection, over-unification problem, figure-set selection bias, six internal inconsistencies, X9 threshold re-examination); further review would surface additional issues.

**Descriptive vs normative status.** The matrix is descriptively reliable in the sense that the cell-status thresholds are stated explicitly and produce reproducible-with-disagreement-at-margins results. The matrix is normatively suggestive in the sense that Type 1 EMPTY cells indicate architectural opportunities only if the framework taxonomy is taken as relevant to medical-discovery AI. Section 11 develops these limits.

Cluster-by-cluster, column-by-column, and overall status distributions presented in Section 3 are from the verified parse.

## 4. Qualitative findings

### 4.1 The X9 finding and what it means

The most consequential single finding from the matrix: in the original matrix, hybrid neurosymbolic architecture is Type 1 EMPTY for all 31 of 31 creativity sub-clusters in medical-discovery AI under strict reading. Held-out validation across 8 systems (supplementary `preregistered_matrix_predictions.md`) preserves the pattern except for one borderline KG-RAG case (RUGGED) classified PARTIAL on application. Medical-discovery AI specifically has not engaged with hybrid neurosymbolic architecture as a deliberate creativity-architectural commitment for any creativity framework cluster, with one borderline case suggesting the X9 threshold around RAG-with-KG-constraint-logic warrants explicit refinement. The finding is not that neurosymbolic AI doesn't exist — it does, with substantial implementations in mathematical theorem-proving, structured reasoning, code generation, and other domains.

**A definitional note on "neurosymbolic."** The X9 finding's strength varies under different definitions of neurosymbolic AI. Under a *strict* reading — neurosymbolic AI requires deliberate symbolic structure with neural components handling within-symbolic-structure dynamics, with the integration designed-in — the finding holds robustly with the RUGGED qualification. Under a *generous* reading — any tool-using LLM counts because LLMs use symbolic tools — Coscientist, ChemCrow, and similar agentic systems would be partial implementations across multiple cells. The matrix uses the strict reading throughout. Readers operating with the generous reading would assign more cells to PARTIAL or INCIDENTAL than the strict reading produces.

The RUGGED case (UCLA Cardiology, 2024; RAG-augmented LLM with KG-grounded retrieval and constraint-application logic preventing hallucination) is the most informative single result of the held-out validation because it forces explicit threshold examination. RAG-with-KG-constraint-logic is closer to neurosymbolic substrate than pure-LLM systems but falls short of full neurosymbolic substrate per the matrix's strict definition. Whether to tighten the threshold (keep RAG-with-KG explicitly INCIDENTAL), loosen it (recognize RAG-with-KG-constraint-logic as PARTIAL universally), or split X9 into sub-categories is an open methodology question. The X9 finding's substantive content — that medical-discovery AI has not built systems where symbolic structure is the primary architectural commitment — survives all three threshold choices, but the universal-Type-1 claim is strict-reading-dependent.

Several observations about the finding's significance:

First, neurosymbolic structure is *architecturally appropriate* for many creativity frameworks. The frameworks specify symbolic structure — Geneplore preinventive structures (partial symbolic skeletons), Wiggins R/T/E formalism (rules, traversal, evaluation as explicit components), Lakatosian protective-belt structure (protected vs modifiable commitments), Levin TAME (scale-appropriate goal specifications), Oulipean constraint families, Bakhtinian distinct perspectives, Hutchins stable artifacts. Neural components handle within-symbolic-structure dynamics. The architectural fit is clean across many sub-clusters; the absence of medical-discovery applications is therefore particularly striking.

Second, the finding's robustness was tested through Phase 3 drill-down work. Each of the 31 X9 cells received an implementation sketch (symbolic component specification, neural component specification, integration mechanism, medical-discovery target) with confidence marker. The confidence distribution across 31 sketches was 18 [high], 9 [medium-high or medium], 4 [medium with caveats]. The drill-down work confirmed that the X9 column's Type 1 status holds at the implementation-sketch level across sub-clusters, not just at the matrix-cell level. The held-out validation provides a different form of robustness test — independent system-level classification rather than within-author drill-down — and yields the threshold-refinement signal noted above.

Third, the finding has positive implications for research planning. A single architectural pattern — neurosymbolic structure — is the densest cross-cluster Type 1 region in the matrix. Investment in neurosymbolic architecture for medical discovery would address Type 1 cells across multiple framework clusters simultaneously. The X9 column's Type 1 density is not noise across uncorrelated cells; it is signal about a coherent architectural opportunity, with the borderline-threshold caveat affecting how RAG-with-KG systems are counted but not the underlying pattern.

Fourth, the finding's caveats. Some individual frameworks within sub-clusters genuinely resist all computational translation (Bergson's *élan vital*, pure phenomenal-state frameworks). The sub-cluster Type 1 status in such cases rests on the *implementable kernel* of one or more constituent frameworks rather than on the cluster as a whole. The drill-down work flags these cases explicitly. The aggregate finding holds; individual-framework analyses within the implementation-sketch work clarify which frameworks within a sub-cluster carry the Type 1 weight.

### 4.2 Two clusters with zero IMPLEMENTED

Cluster C (conditions) and Cluster D Pass 1 (biological scope) each have zero IMPLEMENTED cells across all 14 columns. The findings track different architectural patterns but a common diagnostic.

For Cluster C, the diagnostic is that medical-discovery AI has been *mechanism-focused* rather than *condition-focused*. The conditions that enable creative cognition — motivational (flow, intrinsic motivation, investment theory), constraint-based (Stravinsky, Oulipo, Cage, Eno), dialogical (Bakhtin, Buber, Vygotsky, Rogers), social-structural (Csikszentmihalyi systems, Burt, Sawyer, Rogers diffusion), contemplative (beginner's mind, wu wei, psychedelic-research, hayrah, indigenous), environmental (Hennessey-Amabile, Clark-Chalmers, Galison-Latour), humanistic (Maslow, May, autotelic) — have been treated as deployment context rather than as architectural target. The field has built systems that do creativity-mechanism work (generation, search, refinement, retrieval) under default conditions; conditions-as-architectural-commitment is essentially absent.

For Cluster D Pass 1, the diagnostic is that medical-discovery AI operates predominantly at the *molecular and cellular scales* with minimal architectural engagement with biological scope frameworks. Multi-scale phenomena are ubiquitous in the data; multi-scale agency is not architecturally instantiated. Plant cognition, bacterial-colony cognition, octopus distributed processing, ecosystem-level dynamics — none of these architectural patterns has been brought into medical discovery as deliberate design choice. The convergence-of-creativity finding from animal cognition research (multiple substrates support creativity in nature; current AI commits to one substrate type) functions as a meta-level architectural critique that the matrix surfaces empirically.

The two clusters together specify a substantial architectural research agenda: bring conditions and scope frameworks into medical-discovery AI as primary architectural commitments rather than as deployment afterthoughts. The six hypotheses derived from the matrix address aspects of this agenda; the agenda itself extends beyond what any single research effort can complete.

### 4.3 The B4 finding and the "intensify attention" default

The B4 row contains no deliberate-incubation architecture across the 14 columns examined. The decomposition is: 8 Type 1 cells (X2 generative, X3 retrieval, X5 RL, X6 multi-agent, X7 self-refining, X9 neurosymbolic, X11 foundation models, X13 multi-modal) where the architectural opening is clean; 3 Type 4 honest empties (X1 scoring, X8 KG, X12 causal) where incubation does not translate; 3 INCIDENTAL natural-upgrade targets (X4 evolutionary, X10 active learning, X14 simulation) where pauses functionally exist without being designed as incubation.

The implementation move for incubation is computationally trivial: explicit "loose generation, deferred evaluation" phases. The barrier to implementation is ideological. The dominant trajectory in current AI is *intensify attention* — chain-of-thought, tree-of-thought, self-refine, RLHF, scaling test-time compute. Each of these patterns runs *more* compute on the focused problem. Incubation requires *not running* certain processes, which goes against the field's natural impulse to run more compute.

The B4 finding has three implications. First, it confirms that some matrix Type 1 cells reflect ideological rather than technical barriers. Second, it identifies an architectural pattern (deliberate compute under-utilization at specified phases) that runs against current AI culture but is empirically supported by cognitive-science research (Sio-Ormerod meta-analysis on incubation effects). Third, it suggests an operational positioning angle for incubation-aware architectures: human-AI hybrid scenarios where the human has time-limited engagement and incubation phases run while the human is unavailable, surfacing insights at the start of subsequent sessions.

### 4.4 What the matrix says about current AI architectural homogeneity

A meta-level finding emerges from cross-cutting patterns. Current medical-discovery AI exhibits architectural homogeneity along several dimensions:

- **Few-complex-agents pattern dominates over many-simple-agents.** Multi-agent LLM systems (Coscientist, ChemCrow, similar) use 3–10 agents with rich language interfaces; bacterial-colony-style many-simple-agents architectures are absent. The D1 × X6 Type 1 cell flags this directly.
- **Centralized reasoning dominates over distributed.** Knowledge graphs are designed for query (centralized retrieval) rather than for stigmergic coordination (distributed contribution). The D11 × X8 INCIDENTAL cell flags this.
- **Transformer-everywhere dominates substrate diversification.** Foundation models, generation, multi-agent components, self-refine architectures all use transformer-based neural substrates. The argument from animal cognition that multiple substrates support creativity (corvid, cetacean, octopus, primate, bee, social-insect substrates each producing distinctive creative behaviors) is sometimes adduced as evidence that AI architectural diversification would be productive. This argument should be hedged: the animal-cognition-of-creativity literature is contested (whether all of these substrates "support creativity" depends on creativity definitions, which vary), and the inference from substrate diversity in biology to substrate diversity in AI requires the further premise that substrate matters in similar ways across biological and artificial systems — a premise strong functionalists accept and embodied-cognition theorists reject. The matrix surfaces transformer-monoculture as a *pattern* in current AI; whether the pattern is suboptimal under specific deployment conditions is an empirical question that this paper does not settle.
- **Continuous coupling dominates phased operation.** Generation and evaluation are tightly coupled, with self-refine and RLHF intensifying the coupling. Wallas-style staging and Sio-Ormerod incubation are absent.
- **Strong-prior operation dominates prior modulation.** Generation operates with full prior strength; psychedelic-research-style prior-relaxation as deliberate architectural operation is absent.

The architectural homogeneity is not necessarily wrong — it may be appropriate for specific deployment contexts — but the matrix surfaces it as a *pattern* rather than as inevitability. Sections 5–10 derive six architectural hypotheses from the matrix's empty cells, each composing a coherent design from cells that share architectural commitments. The hypotheses are testable predictions about what could be built and what its distinctive outputs would be, not claims that the architectures will outperform existing systems. They retain the labels P1 through P6 from the project's design phase; "P" stands for "proposal" in the sense of "research proposal" (a hypothesis with a specification for how to test it). Each hypothesis section opens with the discovery-function class where translation is most defensible, identifies the matrix findings driving the architectural specification, presents the component structure, locates the architecture relative to existing work, and discusses validation strategy. One component of one hypothesis (P1's Programme-Progressivity Tracker) is operationalized to depth in supplementary `novel_prediction_operationalization.md` with six worked examples spanning case-types from clean-progressive (imatinib) to degenerative (tau-targeting). Implementation specifications at parallel structural depth for all six hypotheses are in the supplementary `p1_implementation.md` through `p6_implementation.md` files. The implementations have not been built; the hypotheses are predictions for future research, with performance comparison requiring the implementation and validation work specified in Section 11.4.

## 5. Hypothesis 1 (P1): A neurosymbolic core combining Wiggins, Lakatos, and Levin

**Task class.** P1 targets *mechanism reasoning, programme revision, and cross-scale translational risk* — high-uncertainty discovery tasks where the binding constraint is decision quality over evolving scientific programmes rather than predictive accuracy on existing labels. P1 is not a competitor on standard property-prediction benchmarks where data is abundant and bitter-lesson dynamics likely dominate. Its claim is that current systems do not produce concept-evolution traces, cross-scale conflict surfacing, or programme-progressivity contestability traces (use-novelty-annotated revision audit trails with explicit threshold-parameter context); these are the distinctive outputs P1 specifies.

The matrix's findings converge on a single primary architectural specification. Three findings drive this convergence. First, hybrid neurosymbolic architecture is Type 1 EMPTY for all 31 sub-clusters — the densest cross-cluster Type 1 region. Second, D4 (multi-scale biological systems) is the densest single Type 1 row, with Levin's TAME framework under-engaged across columns. Third, PARTIAL cells cluster around iterative-and-adversarial architectures (B5 mechanisms): current AI revises *candidates* extensively but rarely revises *concepts*. The Lakatosian refinement framework — concepts revised through proof-counterexample cycles, with hard-core commitments protected and protective-belt auxiliaries modifiable — has no medical-discovery implementation.

The proposal specifies a neurosymbolic core where the symbolic component represents (a) the rules of the discovery space (Wiggins R/T/E formalism), (b) the protected commitments of the working programme (Lakatos hard core / protective belt structure), and (c) the multi-scale agent structure of biological reality (Levin TAME), with explicit revision mechanisms for all three. The neural component handles within-symbolic-structure dynamics — generation, prediction, similarity, completion. The architecture's distinctive contribution is making *transformational creativity* (Wiggins's sense), *programme revision* (Lakatos's sense), and *multi-scale negotiation* (Levin's sense) first-class operations rather than emergent properties.

**On the choice of Lakatos rather than Kuhn or Feyerabend.** Cluster A5 contains all three paradigm-change frameworks; the proposal operationalizes Lakatos specifically because Lakatos's protective-belt structure (hard core / modifiable belt with explicit progressivity criteria) is substantially more computationally specifiable than Kuhn's gestalt-switch incommensurability or Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism. A Kuhnian instantiation would commit to paradigm-shifts as discrete reorganizations and to incommensurability as architectural limit on cross-paradigm reasoning; a Feyerabendian instantiation would maintain methodological pluralism with multiple distinct rule-systems running simultaneously. Both alternatives are intellectually serious and harder to specify operationally. The Lakatos commitment forecloses framework-specific contributions — particularly the incommensurability work that would matter for genuinely paradigm-shifting medical discoveries — that future architectural work could revisit.

Five components: **Symbolic Rule Substrate (SRS)** holding rules in Lakatosian protective-belt structure with hard core (protected commitments) and modifiable belt (situational auxiliaries); **Neural Generative-Inferential Substrate (NGIS)** as a collection of neural modules operating at scale-specific levels (molecular, cellular, tissue, organ, organism); **Multi-Scale Agent Specification (MSAS)** as a structured fragment of the SRS specifying scale-appropriate agent goals and constraints; **Cross-Scale Negotiation Protocol (CSNP)** running candidates against each scale's agent specification and surfacing conflicts as first-class outputs; **Programme-Progressivity Tracker (PPT)** logging anomalies and proposing revisions when accumulated violations cross threshold, with use-novelty annotations distinguishing predictions made before vs after evidence was available, and with structured-contestability output (the classification travels with its threshold parameters and evidence-corpus context, allowing reviewers to re-run under different parameters rather than producing objective classification independent of reviewer judgment). PPT is operationalized in detail in supplementary `novel_prediction_operationalization.md` with six worked examples spanning the major case-types: imatinib (clean-progressive); trastuzumab (second clean-progressive case with companion-diagnostic precedent); CFTR modulators (productively-pivoting progressive — the canonical case of revision-with-novel-prediction-generation); amyloid-cascade (threshold-contested between theoretically_progressive_empirically_pending and borderline_degenerative); anti-VEGF in oncology (split-outcome by indication, demonstrating PPT's sub-context decomposition); and tau-targeting in Alzheimer's (degenerative classification with traceable evidence — the first negative classification case).

**On MSAS operational realism.** The MSAS architectural commitment requires scale-specific agent goal specifications (cellular agent's goal: maintain physiological homeostasis; tissue agent's goal: maintain morphogenetic target; etc.) tied to validated cell-type-specific or tissue-specific models, of which there are few at the integration level the proposal requires. A first-generation MSAS would specify goal-and-constraint structures that approximate the agent specifications without claiming biological completeness — a "cellular agent" represented as a parametric model of homeostatic regulation in a specific cell type (e.g., hepatocyte HepG2 line) rather than as biologically faithful cell-type-general agency. Two layers warrant distinguishing: (a) the architectural shell — component decomposition, data flow, API surfaces — is fully specified at engineering depth; (b) the biological content required to make MSAS meaningful — the validated per-scale agent models — is not currently available at the integration level required and remains a progressively-buildable contribution that composes with the architecture rather than something the architecture delivers on its own. "Fully specified" refers only to layer (a); conflating the two overclaims what the proposal can do at the time of writing.

Two distinctive architectural commitments warrant attention. First, **cross-scale conflicts as first-class output**: most discovery systems average multi-scale concerns into single objectives or pipeline candidates through scale-specific filters. Surfacing conflicts explicitly supports human deliberation about scale tradeoffs and reveals candidates that fail at one scale before wet-lab investment. Second, **structured-contestability outputs for programme reasoning**: most systems output candidates only; this proposal additionally outputs concept-evolution traces with use-novelty annotations and threshold-parameter-context, supporting human deliberation about whether revisions to the working concepts are progressive (predicting novel phenomena) or degenerative (only accommodating known anomalies). The classification is not objective in the strong sense; it produces auditable, parameter-transparent assessments that constrain the legitimate disagreement space rather than eliminating disagreement. This is unusual as system output and may be the most distinctive contribution.

The closest current systems are scientific discovery agents — Coscientist (Boiko et al., 2023), ChemCrow (Bran et al., 2024), FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024), AlphaEvolve (Novikov et al., 2025), and similar agentic LLM-with-tools systems. These differ from the proposal in specific ways: Coscientist and ChemCrow are agentic and tool-using but their working rules are implicit in foundation-model weights rather than represented as inspectable SRS; they typically operate at one scale (molecular reasoning with chemistry tools) without MSAS; cross-scale conflicts are not surfaced as first-class outputs; programme-progressivity is not tracked. FunSearch and AlphaEvolve are closer in some respects — they use explicit evolutionary or coding-agent search with evaluation infrastructure that approaches programme-progressivity tracking — but operate over mathematical and algorithmic rather than biological structures and lack the multi-scale-agency commitment. The proposal extends rather than replaces these systems: a Coscientist or ChemCrow augmented with SRS (explicit working rules with protective-belt structure), MSAS (multi-scale agent specifications), CSNP (cross-scale conflict surfacing), and PPT (programme-progressivity tracking) is the proposed architecture. Existing agentic infrastructure provides tool-using, foundation-model-coordination, and laboratory-automation interfaces; the proposal adds the neurosymbolic structure that gives them creativity-framework instantiation.

Validation strategies include component-by-component testing (each component independently testable; details in proposals.md and forthcoming experimental-program documentation), retrospective on documented drug failures, and prospective on discovery campaigns. The drug-failure-retrospective framing requires careful candidate selection: not all drug failures are cross-scale conflicts that CSNP would have caught. Torcetrapib failed because of off-target blood-pressure effects (cross-scale: molecular target was correct, organism-scale homeostasis was disrupted) — CSNP-relevant. Fialuridine failed because of mitochondrial toxicity not predicted by available preclinical data (cross-scale: molecular safety profile missed cellular toxicity mechanism) — CSNP-relevant. But many drug failures are simpler (efficacy was overestimated based on biased trial design; safety signals were missed because the trial wasn't powered; manufacturing process couldn't scale) and are not cross-scale conflicts CSNP would have caught. Honest validation requires selecting failures by mechanism (cross-scale conflicts visible in retrospective data) rather than by salience. Operational positioning is community-resource infrastructure (open-source SRS specifications using community ontologies; agent-specifications as community-curated assets) distinct from proprietary efforts that optimize for competitive advantage.

## 6. Hypothesis 2 (P2): A structure-mapping substrate for analogical discovery

**Task class.** P2 targets *analogical drug repurposing and cross-domain mechanism discovery* — tasks where the binding constraint is identifying structural rather than surface similarity between drug-target-disease configurations. P2 is not designed for de novo molecular generation in well-trodden chemical space; that is bitter-lesson territory where scaled generative models likely dominate. P2's claim is for tasks where the analogical move (this oncology mechanism is structurally analogous to that fibrosis mechanism; this autoimmune insight transfers to that immunotherapy context) is the discovery operation.

The B3 sub-cluster (analogy and structure-mapping) has the matrix's largest gap between framework specification and current medical-discovery implementation. Computational structure-mapping has a forty-year tradition (Gentner's SME from 1989, Hofstadter's Copycat lineage, Holyoak-Thagard ACME/LISA, Fauconnier-Turner conceptual blending) with implementations in non-medical domains. Medical-discovery applications: essentially zero. The matrix recorded the row as Type 1 EMPTY across most columns with one PARTIAL (B3 × X3 case-based reasoning, where biomedical CBR exists but operates on surface similarity rather than structure-mapping). Combined with B1 × X9 (Geneplore preinventive structures unimplemented in medical discovery despite being one of the cleanest neurosymbolic targets in cognitive-creativity literature) and A2 × X9 (conceptual blending), the row complex specifies a coherent analogical-discovery substrate proposal.

The proposal specifies four components. **Slip-network of Biomedical Concepts (SNBC)** is a biomedical knowledge graph extended with context-dependent edge weighting — a "kinase inhibitor" has different conceptual neighborhoods in cancer vs fibrosis contexts; the slip-network captures this context-dependence as Copycat's fluid concepts specify. **Preinventive Structure Library (PSL)** is a curated library of partial symbolic skeletons (molecular skeletons with attachment points, partial pharmacophores, mechanism templates) that experienced chemists implicitly use as starting points; the PSL makes implicit knowledge explicit substrate. **Codelet-Workspace Generation Engine (CWGE)** is a multi-agent generation engine where small specialized agents (binding-site-similarity codelet, pathway-overlap codelet, phenotype-signature codelet, clinical-context codelet, regulatory-precedent codelet) compete for attention based on what they're discovering — Hofstadter's Copycat architecture adapted to medical discovery. **Blending Engine (BE)** constructs blended spaces from input domains (cancer-drug mechanism × fibrosis context produces a candidate occupying the blended space neither input space alone supports), with SME-style structural consistency constraints.

Three contributions beyond the field-bridging work: biomedical slip-network specification (specifying which biomedical concept neighborhoods are context-dependent and how — implicit in pharmacology but not formalized as Copycat-style fluid concepts); preinventive structure library curation (a structured library of biomedical preinventive structures has not been organized as architectural component before); cross-domain blending in medical discovery (deliberate architectural commitment rather than accidental property of generation).

Closest current systems are drug-repurposing platforms (Open Targets, Pharos, Connectivity Map, rephetio/Hetionet repurposing). These do something analogy-flavored — finding drugs whose target/pathway/disease-similarity profile suggests applicability to new indications — but with surface similarity rather than structural alignment, static concepts (a "kinase" is a fixed entity), no preinventive substrate, no blending. The proposal extends rather than replaces these systems.

**On the Copycat domain-portability cost.** Hofstadter's Copycat architecture has been ported across several non-medical domains (Tabletop for design analogies, Letter Spirit for typography, Phaeaco for shape recognition, Musicat for music). Each port required substantial domain-specific re-engineering of the slipnet structure and the codelet specifications. The biomedical port the proposal envisions is more substantial than prior ports because biomedical concepts have richer relational structure (multi-scale, multi-mechanism, multi-context) than letter-strings or shapes. The proposal's CWGE specification should be read as architectural sketch rather than complete implementation; substantial domain-specific design work is required before deployment, particularly in specifying which biomedical concept neighborhoods deform under which contexts.

Validation includes retrospective on documented analogical drug-repurposings (thalidomide for multiple myeloma, sildenafil for ED, propranolol for hemangioma — does the system surface the analog earlier than existing tools?), retrospective on cross-domain mechanism discoveries (autoimmune-disease insights enabling oncology immunotherapy), and prospective on active drug-repurposing campaigns. The retrospective tests have a methodological difficulty worth flagging: drug-repurposing successes are over-determined by hindsight, and constructing fair retrospective benchmarks requires careful specification of what counts as "earlier than existing tools" — earlier than what input data was available at what time, with what evaluation infrastructure, with what human-judgment overlay. Honest retrospective evaluation requires curating the input-and-evaluation conditions to match what would have been available pre-discovery, which is a non-trivial historical-research task. The proposal flags this difficulty rather than handwaving it. Operational positioning is community-resource bridging contribution between cognitive-creativity research and medical AI.

P2 is complement to P1 rather than competitor. Substrate sharing is substantial: SNBC and PSL feed P1's SRS; CWGE can serve as one of P1's NGIS modules. Implementation can proceed in parallel.

## 7. Hypothesis 3 (P3): A field-aware neurosymbolic architecture

**Task class.** P3 targets *translational and field-aware prioritization* — tasks where regulatory pathway, clinical-adoption logic, payer coverage, and field consensus state are constitutive of decision quality rather than downstream filters. P3 is most operationally aligned with the kinds of decisions academic medical centers, clinical trial offices, and translational research programs face: which candidates to advance, which trials to design, which mechanism claims to invest in given the regulatory and adoption landscape. P3's claim is not that field-awareness produces better molecules in chemical space; it is that field-awareness produces better decisions about which molecules to advance and how.

The Cluster C population produced the matrix's strongest single-cluster finding: zero IMPLEMENTED cells across all 98 cells in the conditions cluster. Within Cluster C, sub-cluster C4 (social structure and field) produced a row of Type 1 EMPTY cells across most columns. Csikszentmihalyi's systems model — creativity as constituted by individual-domain-field interaction, with field acceptance as constitutive of what counts as creative — has no medical-discovery architectural instantiation. Combined with D7 (organizational structure routing), D8 (cultural-transmission-aware generation), D10 (trust-calibrated hybrid neurosymbolic with complementary cognition), C4 × X13 (regulatory-text-as-modality multi-modal integration), B5 × X12 (Lakatosian programme-evaluation in causal AI), the proposal specifies a discovery system whose design target is field-aware operation rather than autonomous performance.

Five components: **Field Substrate Specification (FSS)** symbolically encodes regulatory logic (FDA approval-pattern rules, draft and final guidance interpretations, advisory committee precedents), clinical adoption patterns (what physicians prescribe, what fits care pathways), payer coverage rules (NCDs and LCDs, formulary tiers), field consensus state (what the field considers high-priority vs underexplored), and patient-community considerations. **Context-Routing Engine (CRE)** classifies discovery problems along Burns-Stalker mechanistic-organic axis and routes operation accordingly — mechanistic for well-charted territory, organic for novel territory. **Cultural-Transmission Layer (CTL)** scores candidates on transmissibility dimensions (articulability of mechanism, simplicity of synthesis, recognizability of precedent, alignment with existing concepts) alongside accuracy. **Trust-Calibrated Interface (TCI)** provides interpretable reasoning traces, calibrated confidence indicators (empirically measured against stratified problem types), explicit override mechanisms, and complementary-cognition routing (decision points routed to whichever agent — human or AI — has more reliable judgment for that decision type). **Field-Claim Revision Ledger (FCRL)** maintains a structured record of how the system's representations of regulatory precedent, adoption logic, and coverage logic change over time, with novelty annotations on each revision (whether the revision was use-novel relative to the available evidence at framework-version time). FCRL is *not* a programme-progressivity classifier for the field as a whole; it produces structured contestability — an audit trail of revisions that supports human deliberation about whether the field is progressive or degenerative — rather than an automated classification.

Three distinctive contributions: field substrate as primary architectural component (vs as filter applied after generation); complementary cognition as design target (vs asymmetric Vygotskian scaffolding); structured field-claim revision tracking with novelty annotations (a field-claim audit trail that supports human deliberation about field-state progressivity rather than automating the classification).

**On applying Lakatos to field-state claims.** Lakatos's original framework concerns scientific theories — research programmes with hard cores of theoretical commitment and protective belts of auxiliary hypotheses. Earlier drafts of P3 included a "PPT-F" component framed as "programme-progressivity for field claims," applying Lakatosian progressivity-vs-degeneracy classification to field-state claims (regulatory precedent interpretation, clinical adoption prediction, payer reimbursement logic) as a working programme. The substantive-vs-decorative philosophical accounting (supplementary `philosophical_accounting.md`) flagged this transfer as decorative at field scope: judging whether a field as a whole is in a Lakatosian-progressive state requires subjective synthesis the system does not perform, and the language of "field-state progressivity classification" implies an architectural capability the proposal cannot deliver. The downgraded FCRL specification in the current draft retains the substantive engineering content (revision tracking with use-novelty annotations on individual field-claim updates — the concept-trace level where the philosophical accounting found Lakatos substantive) and removes the decorative content (the programme-as-a-whole classification). FCRL produces structured contestability, not automated field-state judgment. Readers familiar with Lakatos's original scope will find this the more defensible application; readers attached to a programme-classification reading should consult P1's PPT (Section 5; supplementary `novel_prediction_operationalization.md`) where the classification logic is operationalized at the concept-trace level Lakatos's framework actually supports.

**On field-as-political-process.** P3 treats the "field" as encodable substrate (FDA precedent, clinical adoption, payer coverage). Bourdieu-influenced sociology of science treats fields as sites of struggle for legitimate authority over what counts as knowledge — a richer theoretical content than the substrate framing. The implications: encoding regulatory logic as if it were settled rules misses that regulatory decisions involve substantial unwritten institutional knowledge (which reviewer was assigned, what advisory committee composition, what political context); encoding clinical adoption as patterns misses that adoption is partly contested through clinical-society guidelines, key-opinion-leader influence, and prescribing-pattern path dependencies; encoding payer coverage as policy misses that prior-authorization processes do most of the contestation work. The proposal's FSS as drafted is a useful first approximation but is politically naive in ways that would matter for sustained deployment. Future-work specification should engage field-as-political-process more directly, possibly through human-curator-in-the-loop architecture where the FSS is continuously updated by domain experts who participate in the field rather than just observe it.

P3 is the most operationally aligned of the six hypotheses. Research-operations sensibilities translate directly into FSS specification, CRE routing logic, CTL transmissibility criteria, TCI complementary-cognition design. Specific deliverables include AI-tool-evaluation frameworks for academic medical centers (does this tool surface field-tractability assessment, route appropriately to mode, score transmissibility, support trust-calibration?), and field-tractability assessment for service-line discovery operations.

**On the FSS curation burden.** The proposal as drafted implies that FSS content is extractable from public document parsing (FDA guidance database, OpenFDA APIs, drug-approval-pattern analyses, public coverage policies). This is a substantial underestimate of the curation burden. FDA review decisions involve unwritten institutional knowledge that public documents do not capture; clinical adoption patterns involve prescribing-data interpretation that requires clinical expertise; payer coverage rules are surface-level relative to the prior-authorization processes where most contestation happens. Building a useful FSS for even one therapeutic area requires months of expert curation by people with inside-FDA experience or equivalent depth. "Major therapeutic areas" implies at minimum cardiology + oncology + neurology + immunology + endocrinology + pulmonology + nephrology — years of expert curation. The honest framing of the AIMR open-source FSS deliverable is **bootstrapping a community-curation process**: providing initial seed content, governance structure, and curation tooling so that a community of expert curators can collaboratively build and maintain the FSS over time, not delivering a finished FSS at one moment. This is operationally substantial as a 501(c)(3) deliverable but explicitly recognizes the community-resource positioning.

**On payer-coverage and patient-community components.** Payer coverage is more contested than the proposal acknowledged in earlier drafts. NCDs and LCDs from CMS provide policy-level rules; the prior-authorization processes where most coverage contestation happens are not publicly documented at scale. A useful payer-coverage substrate includes initial-policy-level encoding with explicit gaps for operational reality, plus structured methodology for capturing prior-auth experience as it accumulates. The patient-community-considerations component is currently the proposal's most underspecified element. Patient communities are constitutive of medical knowledge in some domains (HIV/AIDS activism, breast cancer activism, rare disease communities, post-pandemic patient-data initiatives); treating them as one input source among many underweights their epistemic role. The honest framing: this component is a placeholder for future work that requires sustained engagement with patient communities at depth the current proposal doesn't specify. Token patient-input would be worse than no patient-input; the proposal acknowledges this and frames the component as needing community-engagement design beyond the architectural specification.

Closest current systems are discovery agents with regulatory awareness (regulatory-precedent-aware drug repurposing, FDA-trained classifiers for approval probability) — these typically use regulatory information as filter rather than as constitutive design substrate. P3's contribution is the architectural commitment to field substrate as primary component shaping generation, evaluation, and refinement. Validation strategies include retrospective on regulatory-pathway successes/failures and on cross-jurisdictional adoption divergence (does field-aware analysis predict why the same drug has different trajectories in US vs EU vs Japan?), and prospective at partner academic medical centers. Cross-jurisdictional adoption divergence as validation context is genuinely complex and warrants scope acknowledgment: divergence involves regulatory differences, payer differences, clinical-practice differences, formulary processes — multi-jurisdiction FSS specification is substantially more complex than single-jurisdiction FSS, and a meaningful retrospective test would require multi-jurisdiction FSS resources that don't currently exist.

## 8. Hypothesis 4 (P4): A constraint-engineering creativity substrate

**Task class.** P4 targets *early-stage exploratory generation in contested constraint regimes* — discovery campaigns where the choice of constraints is itself the creative act, not the optimization within fixed constraints. P4 is not designed for late-stage lead optimization where constraints are well-specified and predictive accuracy on existing labels matters most. P4's claim is for tasks where constraint structure is uncertain or contested (novel target classes, novel modalities, indications where the relevant biology is poorly understood) and where the system's distinctive output is constraint-to-output relationship maps rather than candidates against fixed constraints.

The C2 sub-cluster (constraint as generative) consistently surfaces architecturally clean Type 1 cells across columns. Stravinsky's productive limitation, Oulipo's systematic constraint exploration, Cage's chance operations, Eno's oblique strategies are each implementable, none implemented in medical discovery. The C2 × X9 cell is the matrix's cleanest "framework-method match that hasn't been made" — neurosymbolic architectures *are* the Oulipean pattern (symbolic constraints over neural generation), and the absence of explicit Oulipean framing in medical-discovery applications is striking.

A second matrix-level pattern: the dominant trajectory in current medical-discovery AI runs in the opposite direction from what these frameworks recommend. Generative molecular design optimizes for output flexibility under minimal constraint (broader sampling, larger candidate spaces, higher temperature). The implicit architectural commitment is that *more* — more parameters, more data, more compute, more sampling — produces more creativity. The constraint-as-generative frameworks claim something different: that *deliberate constraint structure*, including injected randomness and reframing prompts and prior-strength modulation, produces creativity that uniform expansion does not.

Five components: **Constraint Family Substrate (CFS)** represents *parameterized constraint families* (rather than fixed individual constraints), with explicit operations for constraint combination, parameter variation, family enumeration; constraint exploration becomes a first-class operation. **Structural Randomness Injection Layer (SRIL)** performs Cage-style chance operations on architectural choices (prompt structure randomization, reasoning-step randomization, component-routing randomization, attention-pattern perturbation) — randomness at the *structural* level, not at the sampling level. **Oblique-Strategies Module (OSM)** monitors focused-work components for stall signals and injects reframing prompts ("treat this as a different organ system," "what would a regulatory reviewer flag," "what would a patient actually notice") when stall is detected. **Distinct-Voice Constraint Layer (DVCL)** specifies multiple perspective-specific constraint sets — regulatory voice, mechanistic-biology voice, patient-experience voice, clinical-practice voice — each with its own goal-and-constraint set, with voice diversity *protected* from collapsing into a single evaluation function. **Prior-Strength Routing Engine (PSRE)** routes between strong-prior expert mode and relaxed-prior beginner's-mind mode, with deliberate prior degradation in relaxed mode rather than just temperature dial.

Three contributions: constraint families as discoverable substrate (the system's outputs include not just candidates but maps of constraint-to-output relationships); architectural-level chance operations in scientific discovery (Cage's commitment to randomness at the structural level, brought into medical-discovery AI for the first time); voice protection as explicit architectural commitment (preserving Bakhtinian dialogism's diversity-of-perspective requirement rather than collapsing perspectives into combined evaluations).

P4 is the most architecturally counterintuitive of the six hypotheses. Its central commitment runs against the dominant trajectory in current medical-discovery AI — the empirical literature supporting constraint-as-creativity is substantial in artistic, scientific, and engineering domains, with medical-discovery applications plausible but unexplored.

Closest current systems include constrained generation systems (pharmacophore-constrained, scaffold-constrained), multi-objective generation with diverse evaluators, and temperature-modulated generation. P4 differs by treating constraint space as discoverable rather than as fixed, including architectural-level randomness, and protecting voice-specific veto power.

Operational positioning is narrower than P1 or P3 but distinctive — early-stage discovery campaigns where constraint structure is contested are the natural deployment environment. Substantial substrate sharing with P1 (constraint families fit naturally inside SRS protective-belt structure), P2 (CWGE codelets operate within CFS-specified constraints), P3 (DVCL voices include the regulatory and clinical voices specified in FSS). One genuine tension flagged: P4's constraint exploration produces field-novel candidates that P3's field-tractability assessment may flag as low-uptake-probability. Resolution likely involves tunable balance rather than principled hierarchy.

## 9. Hypothesis 5 (P5): A stigmergic distributed neurosymbolic substrate

**Task class.** P5 targets *distributed knowledge construction in consortium-style research, rare-disease research, and long-horizon evidence development* — settings where the discovery activity is itself distributed across many agents (human and AI) over time and where centralized reasoning architectures historically underperform. P5 is not a candidate for single-organization industrial drug development at scale where centralized coordination is feasible and likely preferable. P5's claim is for tasks where the structure of the discovery work is intrinsically distributed (no single actor has all the relevant data; coordination happens through shared artifacts rather than direct messaging) and where current architectural homogeneity in AI does not match this structure.

The D-cluster scope frameworks consistently surface architectural-diversification opportunities distinct from current AI mainstream. Bacterial-colony cognition (D1) uses many-simple-agents with chemical-signal-analog communication. Plant signaling (D2) integrates distributed sensors without central executive. Octopus cognition (D3) operates with semi-autonomous components and limited central oversight. Swarm intelligence and stigmergy (D11) coordinate through environmental modification rather than direct messaging. Current medical-discovery AI exhibits architectural homogeneity: foundation models everywhere, few-complex-agents multi-agent systems, transformer-based components throughout, centralized reasoning with tools.

A complementary matrix finding: knowledge graphs *are* stigmergic substrates without being designed as such. PrimeKG, Hetionet, SPOKE all function stigmergically — agents (researchers, AI systems) modify the shared artifact, prompting subsequent modifications by other agents. The architecture is stigmergic in form, not by design. Engineering the design intention (prompt-richness for subsequent agents, anomaly markers, partial-completeness flags, alternative-interpretation traces) would convert the existing substrate into deliberate stigmergic infrastructure. Adding D5 × X4 (POET coevolutionary problem-solution dynamics, mature in evolutionary computation, absent from medical-discovery applications) extends the architectural-diversification commitment from agents to dynamics.

Five components: **Stigmergic Knowledge Substrate (SKS)** is a knowledge graph deliberately engineered for stigmergic coordination, with anomaly markers, partial-completeness flags, alternative-interpretation traces, priority signals, staleness indicators that prompt subsequent agents toward productive directions. **Many-Simple-Agents Generation Layer (MSAGL)** is a many-simple-agents architecture with hundreds-to-thousands of simple agents, each handling a small sub-problem, communicating through structured signal substrate (high-volume, low-information-per-message), with collective decisions emerging from concentration thresholds. **Distributed-Sensor Integration Layer (DSIL)** is a plant-style distributed integration with many local integration nodes producing local decisions that propagate through signal substrate. **Coevolutionary Problem-Solution Dynamics Engine (CPSDE)** has problem-generators and solution-generators evolving together, with problems designed to be just-beyond-current-solver-capability. **Hutchins Representational-Transformation Protocol (HRTP)** specifies structured transformations between agent classes, preserving cross-checking redundancy that natural-language summarization would lose.

Three contributions: knowledge graphs designed-for-stigmergy in medical discovery (engineering existing biomedical KGs deliberately as stigmergic substrates is novel architectural work); many-simple-agents medical-discovery architecture (architectural pattern exists in non-medical domains; bringing it to medical discovery as deliberate design choice is a substantive architectural reframing); coevolutionary problem-solution dynamics in medical discovery (POET-style architectures have not been brought into medical discovery).

P5 occupies architectural territory distinct from P1, P2, P3, P4 rather than extending the same territory. Substrate sharing is more limited: P5's distributed substrates potentially conflict with the centralized substrates of the other proposals. Whether P5 coexists with or substitutes for the centralized substrates is genuinely open. P5 may be best positioned as alternative architectural commitment for specific deployment contexts (consortium-style research, rare-disease research, distributed-evidence construction) rather than as universal architecture.

Validation includes retrospective on documented community-driven discoveries (PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy across multiple labs, CRISPR through community-shared protocols, mRNA vaccine platform development through long-term substrate), retrospective on rare-disease research where centralized reasoning has historically underperformed, prospective on consortium-style discovery campaigns. AIMR contribution is a stigmergic-coordination annotation schema for biomedical knowledge graphs (reference implementation extending PrimeKG) plus many-simple-agents architectural specification — operationally substantial as community resource.

## 10. Hypothesis 6 (P6): An incubation-aware discovery architecture

**Task class.** P6 targets *human-AI hybrid discovery workflows over long horizons* — settings where humans engage time-bounded sessions and the AI system operates between sessions, with the architectural opportunity being deliberate compute under-utilization at incubation phases rather than continuous intensification. P6 is not designed for autonomous high-throughput screening or for time-pressured optimization. P6's claim is for tasks where the discovery rhythm is intrinsically session-based (academic medical centers; expert-time-limited research programs; clinical-translational work where wet-lab cycles are weeks-to-months) and where deliberate-deferral architecture matches the rhythm.

The B4 row produced one of the matrix's cleanest single-cluster findings: no medical-discovery AI system implements deliberate incubation architecture across the 14 columns examined. The architectural opening is unusually clean — incubation is well-specified by cognitive-science research (Sio-Ormerod meta-analysis gives quantitative experimental constraints and moderators relevant to AI-design hypotheses: substantial preparation, low-demand activity during incubation, divergent rather than convergent problems, longer rather than shorter delays within bounds), the implementation move is computationally trivial (explicit "loose generation, deferred evaluation" phases), and yet no medical-discovery system implements any version of incubation deliberately.

The barrier to implementation is ideological. The dominant trajectory in current AI is *intensify attention* — chain-of-thought, tree-of-thought, self-refine, RLHF, scaling test-time compute. Each of these patterns runs *more* compute on the focused problem. Incubation requires *not running* certain processes, which goes against the field's natural impulse to run more compute. The architectural commitment to deliberate deferral is unusual enough that it warrants its own proposal rather than being absorbed into other proposals as a side feature.

Five components: **Phase Controller (PC)** classifies the system's current operating state and routes between focused-work and incubation phases, with phase transitions as explicit operations. **Loose-Generation Substrate (LGS)** is a generation architecture configured for incubation-phase operation — relaxed validity filters, broad pharmacophore tolerance, no immediate reward gradient — with outputs accumulating in the Incubation Buffer. **Incubation Buffer (IB)** is structured storage for unevaluated candidates produced during incubation phases, persisting across phase transitions with metadata for batch processing. **Tight-Evaluation Engine (TEE)** processes the IB at the start of focused-work phases with full-strength evaluation, distinguishing incubation-survivor candidates that escape the focused-mode filters from candidates that should be filtered. **Cross-Phase Integration Layer (CPIL)** integrates findings across phases into the system's working state, supporting Sio-Ormerod's intervening-generation pattern (compare pre-incubation and post-incubation outputs on the same prompt as a robustness signal).

Three contributions: explicit incubation-phase architecture in medical discovery (architectural pattern exists in computational creativity research; medical-discovery applications absent); Sio-Ormerod-parametrized incubation as architectural specification (treating quantitative findings as design parameters rather than as suggestive findings); operational reframing of compute under-utilization as creativity feature (unusual enough to be a contribution in its own right, applicable beyond the specific architecture).

P6 is the smallest of the six hypotheses in scope but potentially broadest in implication. Its operational positioning is *specifically valuable for hybrid scenarios where compute is available but human attention is bottlenecked* — which describes academic-medical-center deployment accurately. Researchers have limited time to engage with discovery systems; running incubation phases while researchers are unavailable and surfacing insights at the start of subsequent sessions calibrates the system to human working rhythm.

Substrate sharing with P1, P2, P3, P4, P5 is high. Incubation phases apply to P1's NGIS modules (relaxed protective-belt constraints during incubation), P2's CWGE codelets (broader attention during incubation), P3's FSS-checking (relaxed during incubation), P4's CFS exploration (CFS exploration runs naturally during incubation phases), P5's MSAGL agents (no coordination pressure during incubation). P6 wraps the other hypotheses' generation components rather than replacing them.

The proposal's claim — that deliberate phase staging produces creativity benefits over continuous operation — requires substantial empirical demonstration. The cognitive-science literature supports the analog claim for human creativity; the AI-architecture transfer is plausible but unvalidated. Validation strategies include retrospective on documented incubation-style discoveries (Fleming's penicillin, Rosenberg's cisplatin both involved deliberate setting-aside), A/B comparison on standard medicinal chemistry tasks, and prospective on hybrid human-AI campaigns at academic medical centers.

## 11. Limitations and open questions

### 11.1 Methodology and coverage limitations

Several limitations of the matrix-driven analysis warrant acknowledgment.

**Cell statuses as interpretive judgments.** The matrix presents cell statuses as if they were facts about systems; they are interpretive judgments by the matrix's author under stated thresholds. "Coscientist is PARTIAL at A1 × X1" is not an observation; it's a judgment about Coscientist's architectural commitments that Coscientist's authors might dispute. The matrix's authority depends on the threshold definitions being applied consistently and on the framework taxonomy being broadly accepted as relevant. The matrix is the work of a single author throughout; classifications would likely shift at margins under further review, particularly between PARTIAL and INCIDENTAL or between Type 1 EMPTY and PARTIAL where rater strictness varies. The matrix's discriminative power varies by cluster — Cluster A (ontology) has the most heterogeneous status pattern; Cluster C (conditions) has zero IMPLEMENTED cells, so the matrix's discriminative work in this cluster is between PARTIAL/INCIDENTAL/EMPTY rather than including IMPLEMENTED. Cell-by-cell findings should be treated as one author's judgment under stated thresholds rather than as multiply-confirmed observations.

**Matrix granularity and taxonomy.** The 31 sub-cluster × 14 AI-category granularity and the four-cluster A/B/C/D organization are one taxonomic choice among several. Coarser granularity would lose discriminative power; finer granularity would produce a matrix too large to populate consistently. Robustness to alternative organizations is plausible but unverified.

**Coverage of creativity research.** 126 frameworks is substantial but not exhaustive. Notable creativity research traditions are underrepresented or absent — particularly research from non-Western intellectual traditions beyond the indigenous-knowledge-systems framework included in C5. The matrix draws more from theoretical creativity proposals (Wallas, Boden, Wiggins, Hofstadter, Csikszentmihalyi, Levin, etc.) than from empirical creativity research (recent meta-analyses on divergent thinking, on the relationship between creativity and openness, on the empirical robustness of dual-process accounts). Some framework specifications the matrix engages with may not robustly replicate in their original cognitive-psychology contexts. Future revisions should engage with the empirical-creativity-research literature more substantially, particularly where empirical findings would adjust framework specifications.

**Coverage of AI methods.** The 14-category AI taxonomy reflects medical-discovery AI as it exists at the time of writing. Newer methods (e.g., diffusion-language hybrids, world-model-based discovery agents, specialized scientific-reasoning models, recent foundation-model-with-memory architectures) may warrant separate columns in updated versions.

**Coverage of medical discovery domains.** The matrix's primary substrate is drug discovery and repurposing; other medical-discovery domains (diagnostics development, medical-device development, surgical-technique innovation, public-health-intervention design) are partially represented but not centered. Findings should generalize but may have domain-specific exceptions.

**Hypothesis substrate-sharing and post-hoc-construction concern.** The six hypotheses share substrate substantially — P1's SRS is extended by P3's FSS and P4's CFS protective belt; P2's SNBC and PSL feed P1's SRS; P6 wraps generation components across multiple hypotheses. An adversarial reading is that the hypotheses are variations on a single architectural commitment dressed in six framework languages, with the matrix's gap analysis engineered post-hoc to produce six. The honest response: P1, P2, P3 do share neurosymbolic commitments at substrate level and could plausibly be read as one architectural commitment with three distinct framework-instantiations. P4, P5, P6 commit to genuinely distinct architectural moves: P4's constraint-engineering substrate is structurally different from P1/P2/P3's centralized symbolic substrate; P5's distributed many-simple-agents architecture occupies different architectural territory; P6's deliberate-deferral commitment is orthogonal to the others. The matrix's gap analysis surfaces a small number of high-leverage architectural patterns; the hypotheses operationalize these patterns; the patterns share substrate where the underlying commitments overlap and diverge where they differ.

**Drug-discovery cost structure caveat.** The hypotheses optimize generation, evaluation, and reasoning architecture as if these were the bottleneck in drug discovery. They are not. Wet-lab synthesis, assay, and clinical development dominate total drug-discovery cost; computational components are a small fraction of the overall cost structure (precise shares vary by stage and indication). The hypotheses' value comes from producing *better discovery decisions* given the wet-lab cost rather than from reducing the wet-lab cost itself. P1's CSNP that surfaces cross-scale conflicts before wet-lab investment is the hypothesis most aligned with this cost structure; others optimize what isn't the bottleneck and warrant operational realism about how much they would change actual discovery economics.

**Synthesizability prediction limits.** Several hypotheses reference "synthesizability bounds" or "simplicity of synthesis" as evaluation criteria. Current synthesizability prediction models (SCScore, SAScore, retrosynthesis-based prediction) have known systematic failures particularly on novel scaffolds and on multi-step synthesis route prediction. The hypotheses as drafted assume synthesizability prediction infrastructure that has measurable but partial accuracy; honest implementation requires explicit acknowledgment of which infrastructure is assumed and what its known limitations are.

### 11.2 Open questions

**X12 (causal AI) narrowness.** The column has only 1 PARTIAL cell across 31. Whether to broaden the column to "structured reasoning over causal and mechanistic models" is an open design choice with consequences.

**P5 vs centralized-substrate hypotheses.** Whether P5's distributed substrates (SKS, MSAGL, DSIL) can coexist with the centralized substrates of P1 (SRS), P2 (SNBC), P3 (FSS), P4 (CFS) or whether P5 represents a fundamentally alternative architectural choice is genuinely open. Resolution requires empirical investigation during implementation.

**Human-as-first-class-agent.** The hypotheses treat the human user as user-of-system rather than agent-in-system in most cases. Full Engelbart IA-tradition would have humans as first-class agents in the architecture — a stronger commitment than the hypotheses' TCI / human-in-loop framing. Whether to pursue this stronger commitment is an open design choice with consequences for P3's TCI and for how human-AI hybrid scenarios are designed across all hypotheses.

**P3 vs P4 tension.** P4's constraint exploration produces field-novel candidates that P3's field-tractability assessment may flag as low-uptake-probability. The two hypotheses have legitimate competing pressures. Resolution likely involves tunable balance rather than principled hierarchy, but requires empirical investigation during implementation.

### 11.3 The bitter-lesson question

The most threatening objection to the thesis is what Sutton (2019) called "the bitter lesson": that across the history of AI, methods that scale with computation and leverage general-purpose learning consistently outperform methods that encode hand-crafted human knowledge. If the bitter lesson holds at the level of drug-discovery AI, the hypotheses' commitments to symbolic substrates, multi-component architectures, structured representations of creativity-research-derived constructs, and explicit programme-progressivity reasoning may be exactly the kind of hand-crafted knowledge that scaling-and-generality of foundation-model approaches will eventually obsolete. The honest engagement requires taking this objection seriously rather than deflecting it.

**Sutton's argument in summary.** Computer chess, computer Go, computer vision, speech recognition, and natural language processing have each followed the same trajectory: initial systems encoded substantial domain expertise (handcrafted features, expert rules, search heuristics tuned for the domain); these systems plateaued; methods that scaled with computation and learned representations from data eventually surpassed them, often by large margins. The pattern, Sutton argues, is general: we should bet on methods that benefit from compute scaling rather than methods that encode our current understanding of how the problem should be solved. The bitter part is that researchers' specific theories about how the cognitive task should be done turn out to matter less than scaling generic learning over abundant data and compute.

**Why the objection lands on this paper specifically.** The hypotheses encode substantial domain-and-discipline-specific structure. P1's PPT (programme-progressivity tracker) implements Lakatosian reasoning explicitly. P2's SNBC (slip-network bond constraints) encodes Hofstadter-Mitchell Copycat-style fluid concepts. P5's stigmergy commitments implement Holland-Epstein-Sawyer agent-based-modeling architecture. These are exactly the kind of theory-derived structural commitments that Sutton's argument predicts will lose to scale-and-generality. A pure foundation-model-with-tools approach (extend ChemCrow, scale Coscientist, train larger MolFormer-class models on more biological data) is the bitter-lesson-aligned alternative the hypotheses would have to outperform.

**Three honest responses, none of which fully resolve the objection.**

*First response: scale of available data is a binding constraint that doesn't relax with compute.* Sutton's bitter lesson assumes the data substrate is rich enough that learning can extract the relevant structure. In drug discovery, the wet-lab data substrate is ~100x to 10,000x sparser than text-and-image data substrates that powered LLM and computer-vision scaling. A complete bioactivity dataset across all proteins and reasonable small-molecule space is unlikely to be assembled in the next decade; the data simply does not exist at the scale that bitter-lesson-aligned methods historically required. Foundation-model approaches in drug discovery train on ~10^6 to ~10^8 measurements, not the ~10^11 to ~10^14 measurements that LLMs and vision systems train on. This gap is not closeable by compute scaling because the bottleneck is wet-lab assay throughput, not compute. Where data is sparse, structured priors (the kind of priors creativity-research frameworks supply) may provide leverage that learning-from-scratch cannot match in human-relevant timeframes. **This response partly lands.** It applies most strongly to D4-cluster (multi-scale biological) commitments where the relevant data — coordinated measurements across molecular, cellular, tissue, and organismal scales — is genuinely scarce. It applies less to property-prediction tasks where data is abundant and bitter-lesson dynamics may dominate. Honest framing: data sparsity changes the bitter-lesson balance for some tasks but not all.

*Second response: bitter-lesson methods optimize for different outputs than the hypotheses target.* The hypotheses' distinctive outputs are concept-evolution traces (P1's PPT), cross-scale conflict surfacing (P1's CSNP), blended-space candidates with explicit blend-network annotation (P2's BE), programme-progressivity contestability traces (P1's PPT — use-novelty-annotated audit trails with explicit threshold context, supporting human deliberation rather than producing objective classification), and human-AI hybrid reasoning artifacts (P3's TCI). Foundation-model approaches optimize for predictive accuracy on existing labels (binding affinity, ADMET endpoints, trial outcomes). The two are not measuring the same thing. A foundation model that achieves higher AUC on binding-affinity prediction has not solved the problem the hypotheses address (decision-quality reasoning over scientific programmes); it has solved a different problem. Saying "scale wins" requires saying scale wins on the same task; on the hypotheses' distinctive tasks, no foundation-model baseline currently exists to beat. **This response partly lands.** It clarifies that the hypotheses and foundation-model approaches are not directly competing on the same benchmark, which means "scale wins" is not directly applicable. It does not resolve the deeper question of whether the distinctive outputs the hypotheses target *matter* — whether decision-quality reasoning over scientific programmes produces better drug-discovery outcomes than predictive accuracy maximization. The hypotheses' value depends on the answer being yes; this is unproven. Honest framing: the hypotheses operate in measurement space that doesn't currently exist; whether that space matters is the question this paper does not resolve.

*Third response: bitter-lesson dynamics may operate within the hypotheses rather than against them.* Several proposal components are themselves learning-based and benefit from scale: P1's NGIS (neural generation-and-integration substrate) is a foundation-model-class component; P2's SNBC slip networks can be learned rather than hand-engineered; P5's agent-level policies can be RL-trained at scale. The hypotheses are not pure-symbolic alternatives to learning; they are hybrid architectures where learning is the substrate and structure is the protective belt around the learning. The structural commitments determine *how the learning is composed*, not whether learning happens. Bitter-lesson dynamics within each component are compatible with structural commitments at the system level — analogous to how transformers won within deep learning while the deep learning paradigm itself reflected substantial structural commitments (vector embeddings, attention as the binding mechanism, autoregressive next-token prediction as the training objective). **This response partly lands.** It correctly notes that the hypotheses are hybrid not pure-symbolic, and that structural commitments at one level are compatible with bitter-lesson dynamics at another. It does not address the deeper bitter-lesson concern, which is that *any* level of structural commitment may eventually be obsoleted by scale-and-generality at higher levels. If a sufficiently scaled foundation model can implicitly represent Lakatosian programme reasoning, multi-scale agency, and analogical structure-mapping without explicit architectural commitments to them, then the hypotheses' explicit structural commitments are wasted complexity. Honest framing: the hybrid response works if structural commitments operate at the *right* level of the architecture; whether the hypotheses' chosen level is the right level is empirical and unresolved.

**The bitter-lesson question recast.** The honest synthesis is that the bitter-lesson objection is real and the hypotheses do not fully refute it. Three sub-questions decompose the question into parts where evidence can accumulate:

- *Where is data sparse enough that structured priors help?* Multi-scale biological data, longitudinal patient-population data, mechanism-conflict data across trial outcomes, and cross-modality clinical data are all sparse. Structural commitments grounded in these data domains (D4 multi-scale; field-engagement; cross-scale conflict surfacing) are bitter-lesson-resistant on the data-scarcity argument. Property-prediction tasks where data is abundant are bitter-lesson-vulnerable.

- *Are the hypotheses' distinctive outputs (concept-evolution traces; programme-progressivity contestability traces; cross-scale conflict surfacing; blend-network annotations) tasks that matter to drug-discovery outcomes?* If yes, the hypotheses operate in a space foundation models don't currently occupy; if no, the hypotheses are solving a problem nobody needs solved. This is the deepest version of the objection and the paper does not resolve it; it presents the architectural specifications as testable hypotheses about whether such outputs improve discovery decisions.

- *At what level of architecture do structural commitments help vs. cost?* The hypotheses commit to structure at the system-architecture level (component decomposition, explicit reasoning over framework-derived constructs, multi-substrate composition) while leaving learning to operate within components. Whether this level is right depends on empirical comparison; pure foundation-model-with-tools approaches occupy a different point on the structure-vs-scale tradeoff and the comparison is testable.

**Pre-registered prediction about what would falsify the hypotheses against bitter-lesson alternatives.** The hypotheses' value relative to scaled foundation-model-with-tools approaches should manifest most strongly on tasks where: (i) data is sparse, (ii) the distinctive outputs the hypotheses produce are evaluated rather than just predictive accuracy on existing labels, and (iii) decision quality over scientific programmes (rather than per-task prediction) is the outcome measure. On predictive-accuracy benchmarks where data is abundant (e.g., MoleculeNet's well-populated tasks), bitter-lesson alternatives are likely to win and the hypotheses would be wasted complexity. The hypotheses' bet is that the gap between current AI capabilities and what drug-discovery decisions actually require is a gap of *kinds-of-output* and *decision-quality-over-programmes*, not a gap of predictive accuracy on current labels. If this bet is wrong — if better decisions in drug discovery come predominantly from higher-accuracy predictions on existing labels — the hypotheses' added complexity has limited value relative to scaling foundation-model approaches.

This bet is testable, not deflected. The matrix's gap analysis surfaces architectural patterns; the hypotheses translate them; the bitter-lesson question resolves only through implementation and validation. The honest summary: bitter-lesson dynamics are a real risk to the hypotheses; data sparsity, distinctive-output measurement, and decision-quality-over-programmes are the conditions under which the hypotheses are bitter-lesson-resistant; empirical validation in those conditions is the next-stage work.

### 11.4 The experimental program

The validation strategies sketched in Sections 5–10 are aspirational rather than fully designed. This paper produces empirical findings (citation gap, matrix-based diagnostic, held-out validation, PPT operationalization) and derives architectural hypotheses from them; concrete experiment design — falsifiable hypotheses, baseline comparisons, statistical power calculations, pre-registration — is future work this subsection frames.

**For each hypothesis, three validation tiers are appropriate.** Component-level validation (does each component independently produce its specified behavior?), system-level validation (does the integrated architecture produce the claimed creativity benefits?), and operational validation (does deployment in an actual discovery context produce useful outcomes?). The tiers progress from cheapest-to-test to most-expensive; component-level tests are mostly tractable today, system-level tests require partial implementations, operational validation requires partner-institution deployment.

**Benchmark gap.** Current medical-discovery AI benchmarks (TDC, MoleculeNet, similar) are not designed to distinguish creativity-framework-instantiating systems from non-instantiating systems. The hypotheses' architectural commitments produce different *kinds* of outputs (concept-evolution traces, cross-scale conflict surfacing, blended-space candidates, programme-progressivity contestability traces) that current benchmarks don't measure. Designing benchmarks that meaningfully evaluate creativity-framework instantiation is itself a research program. A reasonable starting point: domain-expert-evaluated benchmarks where reviewers blind to system architecture rate outputs on creativity-framework dimensions (does this output show analogical depth? does this output engage cross-scale conflict? etc.).

**Pre-registration and future-work specification.** When validation experiments are eventually designed, pre-registration is appropriate given the post-hoc-construction concern flagged in the limitations. Pre-registering the matrix-derived predictions (which architectural commitments should produce which kinds of outputs) before implementing the hypotheses would substantially strengthen the methodology. Companion forthcoming work will produce structured experimental designs for each hypothesis: falsifiable hypothesis, intervention, comparator, outcome measure, sample-size reasoning, feasibility constraints. The current paper signals these designs as future work rather than presenting them as completed; readers should treat the hypotheses as architectural specifications with validation directions, with the validation infrastructure as the next-stage contribution.

**Replication infrastructure.** Following modern ML methodology recommendations (Pineau et al. 2021), supplementary material should include the matrix data in machine-readable format, the framework taxonomy as structured data, and reproducible analysis scripts for the cell-count distributions presented in Section 3. The current artifact set is markdown documents; producing JSON/YAML supplementary data is the next-stage methodology contribution.

## 12. Deployment paths and operational positioning

The hypotheses' deployment paths span several operational contexts. Three positioning frames are most relevant.

**Community-resource infrastructure.** AIMR (American Institute for Medical Research) is intended as a 501(c)(3) entity for open-source intellectual infrastructure; institutional formation is in progress at the time of writing, so references throughout the paper to "community-resource positioning" should be read as positioning aspiration rather than as institutional fact. Once established, AIMR's potential deliverables include open-source SRS specifications using community ontologies (P1), open-source biomedical slip-network specifications and preinventive structure libraries (P2), open-source FSS reference implementations (P3 — see operational realism notes in Section 7 about this being bootstrapping rather than one-time delivery), open-source constraint-engineering substrate libraries (P4), open-source stigmergic-coordination annotation schema for biomedical KGs (P5), and open-source incubation-aware-architecture wrapper libraries (P6). Deliverables released under permissive licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0) with explicit governance committing to community curation rather than single-institution control are appropriate to the academic-collaborative governance model the FSS curation, SKS annotation schema uptake, and constraint-engineering substrate library all require.

**Academic medical center research operations contexts.** For academic medical centers building research operations infrastructure, the hypotheses support specific applied research collaborations. P3 most directly: a framework for evaluating AI tools the institution is considering or has deployed, derived from FSS/CRE/CTL/TCI/FCRL architecture — does this tool surface field-tractability assessment, route appropriately to mode, score transmissibility, support trust-calibration, maintain a field-claim revision audit trail? P1 supports an architectural framework for integrating multiple AI tools across institutional research operations. P6 supports calibration of existing AI tools to institutional working rhythms (incubation phases between focused-work sessions matched to research-team availability). Operational realism: academic medical center IT and AI deployment processes are typically conservative and the operational tractability of third-party AI architecture in clinical research operations is not high without substantial integration work. Applied research that works *with* existing institutional infrastructure rather than replacing it has lower deployment friction.

**Research-program contributions.** P2 (analogical-discovery substrate), P4 (constraint-engineering), P5 (stigmergic distributed) are more academically positioned — their contributions are bridging research traditions with medical-discovery applications, distinct from immediate applied deployments. These positions are appropriate for academic clinical research center contexts where research-program development is the operational frame.

A minimum-viable deployment path involves: (1) initial open-source release of the FSS reference specification (P3) and the SKS annotation schema (P5), as standalone deliverables establishing community-resource positioning, with explicit framing as bootstrapping community curation rather than one-time delivery; (2) applied deployment of P3's AI-tool-evaluation framework at one or more academic medical centers, validating the field-aware architecture's operational tractability and producing the empirical reliability data needed for trust-calibration claims; (3) prospective implementation of P1's neurosymbolic core in a pilot discovery campaign with a willing partner institution, validating the primary architectural contribution.

## 13. Conclusion

This paper measures a citation gap, infers an architectural gap from it, and derives six testable architectural hypotheses from the inferred gap.

The citation gap is the firmest finding. Across both an n=100 structured systematic survey and a 53,792-paper bibliometric analysis at scale, drug-discovery AI literature shows little sustained engagement with the canonical creativity-research literature: 0/100 papers cite eleven foundational figures at the canonical level; 22 of 53,792 corpus papers (0.041%) cite at least one of thirty extended figures; three substantive engagements concentrated at the biology-AI-philosophy frontier rather than in core methodology; the proportional citation rate is stable at 0.041–0.045% across three corpus iterations of expanding scope despite the largest being 74% larger than the smallest, ruling out the alternative explanation that the engagement rate is an artifact of strict corpus boundary. Kuhn's "paradigm shift" appears in 411 corpus abstracts with zero Kuhn citations — a quantification of vocabulary reinvention without literature engagement. The pattern is not absolute absence of all creativity-adjacent references, but absence of sustained engagement with the canonical creativity-process frameworks (Boden's *Creative Mind* taxonomy, Wallas's stages, Wiggins's R/T/E formalism, Hofstadter/FARG architectures, Csikszentmihalyi's systems model). When AI-for-science researchers reach for creativity-related concepts, they tend to develop operational frameworks de novo rather than building on the canonical creativity-process apparatus.

The architectural gap is structurally inferred. A 31 × 14 matrix mapping creativity frameworks against AI approaches surfaces specific architectural patterns absent from current systems: hybrid neurosymbolic architecture is empty across all 31 sub-clusters under strict reading (held-out validation reproduces the pattern with one borderline exception that forces threshold examination); multi-scale biological agency is the densest single empty row; deliberate incubation architecture is absent across the 14 AI categories. The matrix's classifications are reproducible at the cell level under stated thresholds; the narrative findings reflect interpretive judgment by a single author. The convergent-reinvention objection lands at the level of partial functional reinvention but not at the level of architectural patterns the matrix surfaces as universally absent.

The performance hypothesis is testable but undemonstrated. The six architectural hypotheses (P1-P6) translate the matrix's empty cells into specifiable AI architectures: a Wiggins/Lakatos/Levin neurosymbolic core (P1); a Geneplore/Copycat/structure-mapping substrate (P2); a field-aware neurosymbolic architecture (P3); a constraint-engineering substrate (P4); a stigmergic distributed substrate (P5); an incubation-aware architecture (P6). One component (P1's Programme-Progressivity Tracker) is operationalized to depth across six historical drug-development cases. The hypotheses are predictions for future research, not demonstrations that the architectures will outperform existing systems. Performance comparison requires implementation and validation work this paper specifies but does not execute. Section 11.3 engages the bitter-lesson objection — whether scale-and-generality dominates structured-architecture advantages — without resolving it; the resolution is empirical and comes through implementation.

Together these findings establish a narrower and more defensible claim than the architectural-superiority claim some readers might expect: bringing the creativity literature into the architectural conversation produces specific, specifiable hypotheses whose value is testable rather than established. The bibliometric finding is publishable as a freestanding empirical result. The matrix is a structured method for surfacing architectural hypotheses. The hypotheses are open architectural specifications at structural depth, positioned as community resource for refinement rather than as final designs. Drug-discovery AI's current architectural homogeneity is not necessarily wrong, but it is a pattern rather than inevitability, and the matrix surfaces architectural alternatives that creativity research has specified for decades without the field engaging them. Bringing more of that literature into the architectural conversation is the work this paper begins.
